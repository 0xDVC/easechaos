# Student Adoption Marketing Guide

## Purpose

This guide is for the maintainers of easeCHAOS who want to increase adoption among UMaT students.

The goal is simple:

- get students to try the app quickly
- make class reps comfortable sharing it
- turn exam and timetable stress into repeated usage

This is a short-term campus rollout guide, not a generic brand strategy.

## Product Positioning

easeCHAOS should be presented as a relief from timetable confusion, not as a technical project.

Core message:

> Stop fighting timetable spreadsheets. View your UMaT class and exam schedule in a cleaner, faster, mobile-friendly way.

What students care about most:

- class-specific timetable views
- exam schedule views
- mobile-friendly access
- the ability to save or export schedules
- less zooming, scrolling, and screenshot hunting

## Primary Audience

Focus on these groups first:

1. class reps
2. students in departments with frequent timetable confusion
3. students preparing for quizzes or exams
4. 100 and 200 level students who are more likely to adopt shared tools quickly

Do not begin with "the whole university". Start with a few departments and make the message feel personal.

## What To Emphasize

Lead with pain relief and speed:

- "Check your timetable in seconds."
- "No more zooming into Excel sheets."
- "See your exam schedule on your phone."
- "Use one clean link for your class timetable."

Do not lead with:

- open source
- tech stack
- backend details
- GitHub stars

Those matter for contributors, not for student adoption.

## Main Distribution Channels

### 1. Class Reps

This is the highest-leverage channel.

Why it works:

- students already trust information forwarded by class reps
- class reps already manage timetable communication
- one repost can reach an entire level or department

What to ask from them:

- one message in the class WhatsApp group
- one repost when there is a timetable or exam timetable update
- one reminder during exam week

### 2. WhatsApp Groups

This should be the main channel, not an afterthought.

Use:

- class groups
- departmental groups
- hostel groups
- course group chats

Keep messages short. A long explanation will be ignored.

### 3. QR Posters

Put posters where timetable stress is most visible:

- lecture halls
- department notice boards
- hostels
- library entrance areas
- student study spaces

Each poster should have:

- one strong headline
- one short benefit line
- a QR code
- the product link

### 4. Short Demo Videos

Make 15-30 second videos showing:

1. landing page opens
2. department is selected
3. year is selected
4. timetable or exam schedule appears

Good places to post them:

- WhatsApp status
- Instagram stories
- Telegram groups if relevant

### 5. Timed Reposts

Students care most when the pain is immediate.

Best moments to repost:

- beginning of semester
- after timetable updates
- before quizzes
- during exam timetable release
- one week before exams

## Rollout Plan

## Week 1: Seed Adoption

Goal: get the first visible wave of real student usage.

Tasks:

1. choose 3-5 departments to target first
2. create a QR code linking to the live app
3. prepare one poster image
4. prepare two short demo videos
5. message 10-15 class reps directly
6. get the app shared in at least 5 active WhatsApp groups

Success signal:

- multiple students open the app without needing explanation

## Week 2: Push During Pain Moments

Goal: convert awareness into habit.

Tasks:

1. repost the app when timetable confusion appears
2. repost again when exam conversations start rising
3. collect the most common complaints from students
4. fix obvious friction before expanding distribution
5. ask early users to share the link in one more group

Success signal:

- students begin recommending the app without direct prompting

## Week 3-4: Expand By Social Proof

Goal: make the app feel normal to use.

Tasks:

1. add lightweight social proof to the landing page
2. publish usage-based messages such as "Used across CE, CY, IS, and EL"
3. ask class reps for testimonials or short reactions
4. place more posters in departments already showing traction

Success signal:

- the app becomes a repeated answer to timetable questions

## Suggested Landing Page Improvements

Marketing will work better if the first screen does more of the selling.

Priority changes:

1. add a line that clearly says "Made for UMaT students"
2. add 2-3 screenshots near the top of the page
3. add a short line about exam schedule support
4. add a visible "Install on your phone" prompt for the PWA
5. add a "Share with your class group" button or copyable link
6. add a small feedback link for incorrect timetable data or missing classes

## Messaging Framework

Use this structure for almost every outreach message:

1. state the problem
2. show the shortcut
3. give one action

Example:

> Timetable Excel sheets are stressful to use on mobile. Use easeCHAOS to check your UMaT class and exam schedule faster. Open the link, choose your department and year, and you are in.

## Simple Metrics To Track

Track only what helps decisions.

Recommended metrics:

- landing page visits
- timetable page opens
- exam page opens
- most selected departments
- most selected year groups
- traffic spikes after WhatsApp shares or poster placement

Useful questions:

- which department shares the most?
- are students opening the app but not selecting department and year?
- do exam-related reposts outperform lecture-related reposts?

## Common Mistakes

Avoid these:

1. marketing the project like a developer tool
2. writing long messages for student groups
3. launching to everyone before testing with a few departments
4. ignoring class reps as a distribution channel
5. pushing during quiet periods instead of stressful timetable moments

## Recommended Immediate Next Steps

1. create one poster using Poster Version A
2. generate one QR code for the live app
3. message 10 class reps with the DM copy
4. post the short WhatsApp copy in at least 5 groups
5. collect the first 10 complaints or questions from students
6. improve the landing page based on those complaints
